## Design Context

### Users
Jano is used by OPD doctors who are actively managing their own suite of patients within a single-hospital context. They use the product during live clinic work — under time pressure — and need to move quickly between patient alerts, prescriptions, and prior medical records without losing clinical context.

Core jobs to optimize for:
- Access patient-related alerts at the right moment
- Write prescriptions quickly and confidently
- Access comprehensive previous medical records without hunting

### Brand Personality
Quiet, assured, trustworthy.

The product should feel like it knows the right things at the right time. AI should empower the doctor and compress effort — not elongate the workflow or call attention to itself.

Emotionally, the interface should make the doctor feel: in control, fast, supported without being interrupted.

Three words: **calm, clinical, decisive**

### Aesthetic Direction
Light mode only.

Premium, minimal, clinically calm — with real breathing room and deliberate whitespace. Avoid generic AI-generated health-tech UI: over-segmented dashboards, excessive boxed widgets, ornamental noise, and cluttered "overview screen" mentality.

Active palette: Crimson, Charcoal, Snow, Success Green, Stroke, Oslo, Warm Grey, Intermediate Yellow.

**Anti-references:**
- Too many equal-weight modules competing at once
- Mechanical card grids and quick-access blocks that feel templated
- Visual density without hierarchy
- Flows that look like polished wireframes rather than designed products

### Design Principles
1. **Design for clinical command, not feature display.** Every screen should help the doctor act decisively, not browse a dashboard of options.
2. **Keep AI quiet.** Intelligence surfaces as timely relevance, summarization, and acceleration — never verbose or attention-seeking.
3. **Use whitespace as prioritization.** Breathing room creates confidence and readability, not emptiness.
4. **Make the right thing obvious at the right time.** Alerts, records, and prescription actions appear with clear hierarchy and minimal interaction cost.
5. **Avoid templated health-tech patterns.** No card farms, no decorative slop, no AI-aesthetic shortcuts, no visual clutter that weakens trust.

### Structural Rules
- Patient home: attention items → quick actions → clinical timeline. Patient identity block stays above all sections.
- Attention stack is a finite review queue, not an endless carousel. Swipe confirmation must be explicit.
- Internal notes in care threads should be clearly distinguished without being loud.
- New object-creation flows should default into the primary workflow directly when one path is clearly dominant. Avoid intermediary option sheets that only add friction.
- Overlays, previews, and modals must stay bounded within the simulated mobile device frame. Do not anchor product UI to the browser viewport.
- Read-heavy clinical detail surfaces should prefer centered in-phone modal sheets with balanced side margins and full use of available width.
- Print previews should simulate a mobile-contained document experience first, even when the underlying artifact is a full prescription sheet.
- AI suggestion treatments must stay compact and quiet: small inline icon, explicit label, and token-based visual emphasis only. No oversized AI badges or off-brand gradients.
