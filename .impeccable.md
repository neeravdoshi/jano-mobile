## Design Context

### Users
Jano is used by OPD doctors who are actively managing their own suite of patients within a single-hospital context for now. They are using the product during live clinic work, under time pressure, and need to move quickly between patient alerts, prescriptions, and prior medical records without losing clinical context.

The first jobs to optimize for are:

- access patient-related alerts at the right moment
- write prescriptions quickly and confidently
- access comprehensive previous medical records without hunting

The broader workflow will expand over time, but the product should already feel credible for these core OPD tasks.

### Brand Personality
Quiet, assured, trustworthy.

The product should feel like it knows the right things at the right time. AI should empower the doctor and compress effort, not elongate the workflow or call attention to itself.

Emotionally, the interface should make the doctor feel:

- in control
- fast
- supported without being interrupted

### Aesthetic Direction
Light mode only for now.

The visual direction should be premium, minimal, and clinically calm, with real breathing room and deliberate whitespace. The design should avoid looking like generic AI-generated health-tech UI, especially over-segmented dashboards, excessive boxed widgets, ornamental noise, or a cluttered “overview screen” mentality.

The active color direction should follow the Jano working-file palette: `Crimson`, `Charcoal`, `Snow`, `Success Green`, `Stroke`, `Oslo`, `Warm Grey`, and `Intermediate Yellow`.

The attached anti-reference reinforces what to avoid:

- too many equal-weight modules competing at once
- mechanical card grids and quick-access blocks that feel templated
- visual density without hierarchy
- flows that look like wireframes that were polished, rather than products that were designed

References will be provided microflow by microflow and should be treated as craft anchors as they arrive.

### Design Principles
1. Design for clinical command, not feature display. Every screen should help the doctor act decisively, not browse a dashboard of options.
2. Keep AI quiet. Intelligence should surface as timely relevance, summarization, and acceleration, never as verbose or attention-seeking interface behavior.
3. Use whitespace as prioritization. Breathing room should create confidence and readability, not emptiness or wasted space.
4. Make the right thing obvious at the right time. Alerts, records, and prescription actions should appear with clear hierarchy and minimal interaction cost.
5. Avoid templated health-tech patterns. No generic card farms, no decorative slop, no “AI app” aesthetic shortcuts, and no visual clutter that weakens trust.

### Current Structural Rule
For the patient home screen, the structural order should be:

- then the items that currently need intervention or review
- then quick actions
- then the patient timeline / clinical history

The patient identity block stays above those sections, directly under the shell header.

Do not let queue-like overview surfaces or generalized dashboard summaries overtake that hierarchy on this screen.

### Current Interaction Notes
- The attention stack is acceptable as a triage surface for medical data only if it behaves like a finite review queue, not an endless carousel.
- Swipe confirmation should be explicit. Visual progress and dismissal threshold must match, or the interaction will feel untrustworthy in a clinical context.
- Background attention cards should signal depth, not leak half-readable medical content. Blank structural previews are preferable to misaligned partial text.
- Homepage clinical history should privilege readability and chronology over decorative density. If card actions squeeze copy into a narrow column, move the CTA rather than preserving a clever layout.
